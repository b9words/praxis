### **The Crucible Framework: A 3-Step Guide to Creating Case Studies**

This framework ensures every case study is grounded in a real-world problem, targets a specific skill, and is engineered for a powerful learning experience.

#### **Step 1: The Core — Define the Crucible**

This is the strategic foundation of the case study. Get this right, and the rest will follow.

**1.1. Select the Skill to Forge:**
*   Start by choosing **one** concrete, transferable skill from your master list that this case will be designed to build. Do not try to teach more than one primary skill per challenge.
*   **Examples:** `Asymmetric Warfare`, `Second-Order Thinking`, `Working Capital Management`, `Leading a Turnaround Culture`.

**1.2. Identify the Historical Anchor:**
*   Ground the case in a real-world business dilemma. It doesn't have to be a famous event, but it must be a real, recurring **problem-pattern** that leaders face.
*   **Process:**
    1.  **Start with the problem, not the company.** Instead of "Let's do a case on Apple," start with "Let's do a case on the problem of cannibalizing your own successful product."
    2.  **Find the historical parallel.** Ah, Apple's decision to launch the iPhone while the iPod was dominant is a perfect anchor for this problem.
    3.  **Fictionalize for focus.** We will create a fictional company, "Melody Inc.," facing the exact same *type* of dilemma. This gives you the freedom to engineer the data and remove the hindsight bias of the real event.

**1.3. Define the Central Tension:**
*   A great case study is not a problem with a right answer; it is a dilemma with two or more valid, competing truths. Explicitly define the core trade-off the user will be forced to confront.
*   **Formula:** *"The user must choose between (Valid Option A) which optimizes for (Benefit A) but risks (Cost A), and (Valid Option B) which optimizes for (Benefit B) but risks (Cost B)."*
*   **Example (Melody Inc.):** *"The user must choose between **(launching the integrated 'Symphony' device)** which optimizes for **(long-term market dominance)** but risks **(destroying our most profitable hardware division)**, and **(launching a next-gen 'Concerto' music player)** which optimizes for **(short-term profit and stability)** but risks **(being made obsolete by a competitor)."***

---

#### **Step 2: The Content — Build the Simulation**

This is the development phase where you create the assets for the challenge.

**2.1. Choose the Challenge Type:**
*   Select the **one** challenge type from your list of 15 that is best suited to simulating the core skill. The skill should dictate the format.
*   **Example:**
    *   To teach **Asymmetric Warfare**, a `Pre-Mortem Red Team` is perfect.
    *   To teach **Working Capital Management**, a `Redline Negotiation` of payment terms is ideal.
    *   To teach **Leading a Turnaround**, a `"Turnaround" Town Hall Script` is the right tool.

**2.2. Assemble the "Case File" Dossier:**
*   Create the set of realistic, conflicting source documents the user will need. A good dossier creates ambiguity and forces the user to synthesize information, just like in the real world.
*   **Checklist for a Strong Dossier:**
    *   **[✓] Quantitative Data:** A spreadsheet, a financial summary, a market research report with hard numbers.
    *   **[✓] Qualitative Context:** An internal memo, a collection of news articles, a transcript of a customer interview.
    *   **[✓] Conflicting "Human" Input:** A panicked email from your Head of Sales, a skeptical memo from your risk-averse CFO, an overly optimistic report from your engineering team.

**2.3. Write the Prompt & Define the Output:**
*   Write a clear, concise prompt that outlines the user's task.
*   Crucially, define the specific **artifact** the user must produce. This gives them a concrete goal and makes their performance measurable.
*   **Example Prompt (Melody Inc.):**
    *   **Skill:** Cannibalization Strategy.
    *   **Challenge Type:** The "Managed Decline" Plan.
    *   **Artifact:** *"Your task is to produce a 2-page operational plan for the 'Concerto' division for the next 24 months. The plan must detail your proposed budget, headcount adjustments, and key performance indicators (KPIs) for maximizing cash flow during this managed decline."*

---

#### **Step 3: The Feedback — Engineer the Learning**

This is the quality control and feedback loop.

**3.1. Create the Scoring Rubric:**
*   Before the case is finished, define "what good looks like." Create a simple rubric that the AI Coach will use to assess the user's submitted artifact. The rubric should directly measure the target skill.
*   **Example Rubric (Melody Inc. Plan):**
    *   **Financial Acumen (1-5):** Does the plan's budget realistically maximize cash flow?
    *   **Strategic Thinking (1-5):** Does the plan effectively balance profit with the need to support the new 'Symphony' project?
    *   **Leadership & Communication (1-5):** Is the plan structured and written in a way that would realistically align the team, or would it cause panic?

**3.2. Conduct the "Red Team" Review:**
*   Before publishing, have another team member review the case study with a critical eye. This is your internal quality control.
*   **Review Checklist:**
    *   **Is the central tension clear and compelling?** Is there a genuinely difficult choice to be made?
    *   **Is the skill being tested obvious?**
    *   **Is any of the provided data a "red herring"?** (Good! This adds realism).
    *   **Is the challenge winnable?** Is there enough information for a smart user to produce a high-quality artifact?
    *   **Is it boring?** If so, why? How can we raise the stakes?

By following this simple, three-step framework, you can ensure that every case study you create is strategically sound, well-constructed, and, most importantly, a powerful tool for forging real executive skills.
